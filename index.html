General Information About Treaty 1

Treaty 1 is an agreement between The Crown and Anishinaabe and Muskegon Cree peoples. The Indigenous people of that area had to give some of their land to The Crown, in return of money and farming provisions. Some things they were expected to recieve included payments, reserves, tools/livestock, and education. It was signed in Lower Fort Garry in Winnipeg, Manitoba. Treaty 1's negotiations started on July 27, 1871 in Lower Fort Garry, and was later signed on August 3rd, 1871.

The Canadian Encyclopedia

The First Nations communities that are involved and represented by this Treaty include:

  • Peguis First Nation
  • Sagkeeng First Nation
  • Brokenhead Ojibway Nation
  • Roseau River Anishinaabe
  • Long Plain First Nation
  • Sandy Bay Ojibway First Nation
  • Swan Lake First Nation

Historic Terms of Treaty 1 (What was Included)

The biggest reason why Treaty 1 was signed was because both sides thought the Treaty was for their benefit. The Indigenous people were looking for a way to adapt to the settlers. They wanted to survive and protect their traditional lands, hence why this agreement was significant to them. For The Crown, they wanted a way to have control over the Indigenous people and their land. Queen Victoria was referred to as the "Great Mother" in the Treaty. She wanted to deal fairly and desired for the Indigenous people to adopt more agriculture. The represenative that was responsible for signing Treaty 1 for The Crown was the Governor of Manitoba, Adams George Archibald. Archibald wanted to ensure and "facilitate" the European settlements, while also focusing on the assimilation of Indigenous people.

The Canadian Encyclopedia

During the negotiating, Archibald introduced the idea of "reserves". He was still annoyed that the Indigenous people would still have freedom over their land, which he didn't want. To limit the expression of Indigenous people's way of life, it was stated that these lands would be taken from the reserves in the future when they were "needed for use".

The Canadian Encyclopedia

In the Treaty, it was stated that each Indigenous Band was to get a reserve large enough that it could provide 160 acres of land for each family of five. Smaller and larger families would get the acres in like proportions. Also, every Indigenous person was going to recieve a one-time gratuity of 3 dollars. Equally, they would get a yearly annituity of 15 dollars for each family of five. Other things that the Indigenous reserves would get included [residential] schools and no availibility for liquor. If Indigenous people wanted these reserves, they had to "cede, release, surrender, and yield" tracts of land to the Queen.

The Canadian Encyclopedia

Outside Promises of Treaty 1

There were many terms of the Treaty that weren't fulfilled. These terms are called "Outside Promises". The Crown was required to provide clothing, animals, and tools for farming to the Indigenous people. In February of 1872, when these provisions were still not given, the Indigenous people complained of how the Crown was not loyal to the Treaty. When Archibald found out, he blamed Commissioner Simpson, as was responsible for not giving the promised items. This problem was later solved when Simpson got replaced by Joseph Provencher as the new Indian Commissioner. On April 30 of 1875, the issue was solved and everyone got the things they were promised. Additionally, the gratuity increased from 3 dollars to five, and the chief would also get 20 dollars.

The Canadian Encyclopedia

How Treaty 1 is different from other Treaties in Canada

Treaty 1 is different from other Treaties in many ways. Firstly, Treaty 1 is focused on the exchanging of land and tools. It doesn't state specific activities or rights the Indigenous people had on those lands. Other Treaties focus on hunting and fishing rights, while Treaty 1 was for education, land, and other provisions. Another way Treaty 1 is different from other Treaties is the geographical size. Some Treaties have a larger area, covering many provinces. Treaty 1 only affects the majority of Southern Manitoba.

Government of Canada

How Treaty 1 is Being Implemented Today

After all that has happened, we acknowledge that Treaty 1 wasn't the best agreement, due to the lack of understanding between both parties. In 2006, commissions were set up to understand the relations of activities and historic treaties. Today, the education of Treaty 1 is being implemented in our schools, businesses, and economies. Everyday we acknowledge the land of Treaty 1, it's people, and how it's resources are given to us. This reconciliation is all for the better relationships between the people.
